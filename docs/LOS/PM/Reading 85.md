### <span style="color: #1565C0;">Part 1: The Process and The People</span>

Before we can build a portfolio, we must understand *why* we're building it and *for whom*.

#### <span style="color: #6A1B9A;">1.1 The Portfolio Approach (LOS 85.a)</span>

The very first lesson a samurai learns is to never be caught off guard. The **portfolio perspective** is your armor.

  * **The Flawed Approach:** Evaluating an investment in isolation (e.g., "This stock is great, I'll put all my money in it\!"). This is reckless, as it ignores how the asset *interacts* with your other holdings.
  * **The Samurai Approach:** Evaluating individual investments by their **contribution to the risk and return of the entire portfolio**.
  * **The Grand Prize: Diversification:** This is your core defense. By combining assets that are not perfectly correlated, you can **reduce your total portfolio risk without sacrificing expected return**. This is the closest thing to a "free lunch" in finance.
      * **Diversification Ratio:** We can measure this benefit. It's the ratio of a portfolio's risk (standard deviation) to the weighted average risk of the individual securities. A lower ratio means greater risk reduction.
      * **A Warning:** Diversification works best in *normal* markets. During a financial crisis, correlations often rise, and this benefit can be reduced.

#### <span style="color: #6A1B9A;">1.2 The 3-Step Portfolio Management Process (LOS 85.b)</span>

Every mission, every portfolio, follows a disciplined, three-step loop.

1.  **The Planning Step:** This is where you create your battle plan, the **Investment Policy Statement (IPS)**. This is the most crucial step. You analyze the client's risk tolerance, return objectives, and all constraints (covered in Reading 86).
2.  **The Execution Step:** This is where you act on the plan.
      * **Asset Allocation:** You decide the broad mix of assets (e.g., 60% stocks, 40% bonds) based on the IPS and your market forecasts (a "top-down" view).
      * **Security Selection:** You choose the specific securities *within* each asset class (e.g., which stocks to buy) based on valuation (a "bottom-up" view).
3.  **The Feedback Step:** The battle is never over. You must constantly monitor and adapt.
      * **Monitoring & Rebalancing:** You watch the portfolio and bring it back in line with the IPS if the allocations drift too far.
      * **Performance Evaluation:** You measure your results against the benchmark defined in the IPS to see if your strategy is working.

#### <span style="color: #6A1B9A;">1.3 Types of Investors (LOS 85.c)</span>

You must know who you are fighting for. Every client is different.

  * **Individual Investors:** People saving for various goals like retirement, a house, or their children's education. Their characteristics (risk tolerance, time horizon, etc.) are unique to each person.
  * **Institutional Investors:** These are large organizations managing pools of money.
      * **Endowments:** Funds that support a *specific purpose* (like a university) indefinitely. They typically have a **long time horizon** and **high risk tolerance**.
      * **Foundations:** Funds established for *charitable* purposes. Similar to endowments, they have a **long time horizon** and **high risk tolerance**.
      * **Banks:** Manage money to earn a spread. They have **low risk tolerance** and **high liquidity needs** to meet depositor withdrawals.
      * **Insurance Companies:** Also have **low risk tolerance** and **high liquidity needs** to pay claims.
      * **Defined Benefit Pension Plans:** (See below). Typically **high risk tolerance** and **long-term**.
      * **Mutual Funds:** (See below). Characteristics *depend on the fund's stated goal*.
      * **Sovereign Wealth Funds:** Government-owned investment funds.

#### <span style="color: #6A1B9A;">1.4 Pension Plans: DC vs. DB (LOS 85.d)</span>

This is a critical, highly testable distinction. Who bears the risk?

  * **Defined Contribution (DC) Plan:**
      * **What it is:** The firm contributes a certain amount (e.g., 5% of your salary) to your retirement account *each period*.
      * **Who bears the risk:** **The employee**. You choose the investments, and your final retirement benefit depends entirely on how well your investments perform. The firm makes *no promise* about the final value.
  * **Defined Benefit (DB) Plan:**
      * **What it is:** The firm *promises* you a specific payment *after you retire* (e.g., 2% of your final salary for each year of service).
      * **Who bears the risk:** **The employer** (plan sponsor). The firm must manage a pool of assets to ensure it can meet these future promises. If the investments do poorly, the *firm* is on the hook to make up the difference.

### <span style="color: #1565C0;">Part 2: The Industry & Investment Vehicles</span>

This is the world you are entering‚Äîthe firms that manage money and the products they create.

#### <span style="color: #6A1B9A;">2.1 The Asset Management Industry (LOS 85.e)</span>

  * **Buy-Side vs. Sell-Side:**
      * **Buy-Side:** The "buyers" of investments. These are asset managers, pension funds, etc., that manage money for clients.
      * **Sell-Side:** The "sellers" or service providers. These are brokers and investment banks that sell securities and research to the buy-side.
  * **Active vs. Passive Management:**
      * **Active:** The manager is actively trying to *beat* a benchmark (e.g., the S\&P 500) through security selection or market timing. This costs more (higher fees).
      * **Passive:** The manager tries to *replicate* a benchmark (e.g., an S\&P 500 index fund). This is cheaper (lower fees). The market share for passive management has been growing.
  * **Traditional vs. Alternative:**
      * **Traditional Managers:** Focus on stocks and bonds.
      * **Alternative Managers:** Focus on private equity, hedge funds, real estate, commodities, etc.. Profit margins here are typically higher.

#### <span style="color: #6A1B9A;">2.2 Pooled Investments: The Army (LOS 85.f)</span>

Pooled investments are vehicles that combine funds from many investors into a single portfolio.

  * **Mutual Funds:** The classic example.

      * **Open-End Funds:** The most common type. Investors buy/sell shares *directly from the fund* at the **Net Asset Value (NAV)** at the end of the trading day. The fund grows or shrinks as investors move money in and out.
      * **Closed-End Funds:** The fund has a *fixed number of shares* that are issued in an IPO and then trade on a stock exchange *like a stock*. The price is set by the market and can be at a **premium or discount** to its NAV.

  * **Types of Mutual Funds:**

      * **Money Market Funds:** Invest in short-term, low-risk debt.
      * **Bond Funds:** Invest in fixed income, sorted by maturity, credit quality, etc..
      * **Stock Funds:** Can be **index funds** (passive) or **actively managed** (growth, value, etc.).

  * **Comparing Other Pooled Investments:**

      * **Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs):** Like a mutual fund, but trade *throughout the day* on an exchange like a closed-end fund. They are typically low-cost, tax-efficient, and often passively managed.
      * **Separately Managed Accounts (SMAs):** A portfolio owned by *one single investor* and managed to their specific needs. No shares are issued. For high-net-worth individuals.
      * **Hedge Funds:** Lightly regulated pools for *qualified* (wealthy) investors. Use complex strategies, leverage, and short-selling. Have high minimum investments.
      * **Private Equity / Venture Capital:** Invest in private companies, often taking an active role in management, with the goal of selling them later.
